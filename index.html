Visualizing Gender in Comic Books

Gender Disparity in Comics

Comics are mostly male-dominated. Women are usually token characters, and when portrayed, are over-sexualized and stereotyped; minority groups make even fewer appearances. Recent studies indicate that exposure to inaccurate representation of women in superhero comics decreased egalitarian beliefs in gender roles and lowered body esteem. Also, most of these representations are stereotyped based on the American perspective. In this age, when comic books and superhero movies are billion-dollar commodities, we aim to study Gender Representation in Comic books. By visualizing the disparity in Power, Moniker, Allegiance etc, we hope to understand the themes and beliefs around gender in Comic Books. We hope to better understand gender representation and spark meaningful discussions about the importance of accurate representation.

Men will be men; women will be girls

Comparing the balance of gendered names

Comic characters have titles such as Superman, Batman, Wonder Woman, Ms. Marvel, Spider-Man etc. However, the usage of “woman” and “man” in the titles are not balanced, male characters are given “man” far more frequently than female characters are given “woman”. On another note, female titles with “Girl” which is like a diminutive version of a woman appear more compared to male titles that have “Boy”. The “Girl” titles are the younger, or infantilized versions of another name. Females are more likely to be given a name that may make her seem weak, less dangerous, less aggressive and not on equal footing with male characters. The Wordle shows an interesting imbalance between many gendered naming pairs.

Gendered Names

If we compare the use of diminutive names for male and female characters, we see that 28.5 percent of female characters and 12.6 percent of male characters fit in the category. Females are more than twice as likely to be given a name that may make her seem weak, less dangerous, less aggressive and not on equal footing with male characters.
